Strawman 4.0

A lesson structure built around what the product can carry, and what only a teacher can do.

Great Minds' Bird's Eye View vision centers on two things: students engaging in grade-level content, and teachers doing the work only teachers can do — building relationships, facilitating discourse, developing mathematical identity, and managing a productive classroom.

Strawman 2.0 surfaced a real problem: teachers are overwhelmed. Strawman 4.0 responds by shifting responsibility for skill-building, practice management, and feedback delivery onto the product — freeing teachers for that irreplaceable human work, and giving explicit support where Strawman 2.0 found the most friction: routine fidelity, pacing, and responding to data.

Teacher Preparation, Planning, and Implementation

Three problem areas emerged from Strawman 2.0 and Eureka Math² user feedback: effective instructional decision-making, Teacher Edition volume and usability, and lack of coaching. The overlap in these problem areas is significant. Teachers cannot make good instructional decisions in part because materials are challenging to navigate; coaching gaps compound both issues. The problems and hypotheses that follow are consolidated into clusters that reflect how the issues interact, followed by a student-centric reframe of each.

Across all clusters, the risk converges on the same place. When the curriculum consistently asks more of teachers than they can reliably deliver, students bear the cost. That cost is not evenly distributed. Students whose teachers are newest to HQIM, under the most workload pressure, or in the least-resourced schools experience the widest gap between what the curriculum intends and what students receive.

The goal of MH2 is not only better teacher decision-making but also to ensure that every student has access to a coherent, well-supported learning experience that does not depend on whether their teacher had 10 minutes or two hours to prepare.

Problem: Materials are too hard to use, so teachers improvise.

Teacher-facing materials currently in use are large, fragmented, and challenging to navigate. Teachers operating under real workload constraints cannot engage deeply with the curriculum as written, leading to surface-level familiarity or improvised departures from the intended design. The essential instructional priorities are buried inside dense, multi-page teacher editions that can require significantly more prep time than teachers have available (i.e., 7+ hours each week). Without support, teachers are left to make instructional choices on their own, leading to inconsistent delivery of the intended mathematical experience.

Student Experience
Students experience fragmented and incoherent lessons. The quality of their mathematical experience depends heavily on how much time their teacher had to prepare to teach the lesson. When teachers improvise, their in-the-moment decisions typically lead to a less rigorous learning experience for students because most mathematically demanding parts of a lesson (the discourse, the summarization, and the productive struggle) are the first to go. Students in under-resourced schools, where teacher workload is highest, absorb the biggest losses.
Hypothesis
If MH2 materials are redesigned to reduce the time and cognitive load of preparation, then teachers can implement with integrity and students get a consistent, mathematically rich experience regardless of how much prep time their teacher had. This means:
  • Lesson prep capped at 10 minutes by prioritizing teacher-facing content and AI-assisted planning tools like Co-teach.
  • A concise lesson-level "mathematical intent brief" (core idea, misconceptions, one or two essential moves) surfaced at the top of every lesson, possibly in video that can be watched/listened to on the way to work.
  • Slide decks organized by instructional moment (warm up, new learning with summarization, and practice) rather than curriculum-author conventions. These decks support lesson pacing and content delivery through dynamic animations and explicit teacher and student directions.
  • Explicit adaptation guidance: must-do vs. can-extend components, time-variant pathways for 45-, 60-, and 75-minute class blocks.
  • Minimal number of transitions between lesson components, instructional modalities, materials, or content types. Each transition requires time, attention, and organization, which can interrupt the flow of learning.

  • Measuring student engagement, communication, problem-solving (observation protocol)
Problem: Teachers cannot respond to student needs in real time.

Even when teachers have prepared well, in-the-moment instructional decisions are hard. Deciding when to scaffold, when to press for deeper reasoning, and when to step back requires simultaneously interpreting student thinking, recalling pedagogical strategy, and making a judgment call under real-time pressure. Teachers also lack usable data from practice and assessment components to inform next-step decisions because curriculum and assessment systems are disconnected. The result is over-scaffolding, premature answer-giving, and missed opportunities to respond to the specific confusion a student is experiencing. For some students, the result is no teacher response at all.

Student Experience
Students do not consistently get the support they need when they need it, whether a teacher intervenes too soon and removes their struggle, or never reaches them at all. Over time, students may come to see confusion as something to wait out rather than work through, since neither outcome shows them what productive struggle looks like. Students who need the most practice with high-cognitive-demand problems end up with the least genuine engagement with them, whether through dilution or through never reaching them. Over time, this shapes how students understand themselves as math learners.
Hypothesis
If MH2 embeds just-in-time instructional support directly within the teacher-facing lesson experience, teachers will make higher-quality in-the-moment decisions and students will more consistently receive the support they need when they need it. This means:
  • Problem-specific coaching frameworks that surface tiered guidance (press, scaffold, or step back) anchored to each problem's intended cognitive demand.
  • Structured and visible supports (teacher prompts, live coaching cues) that increase student-to-student talk and use of mathematical reasoning language in discourse.
  • Classroom routines embedded as integral, time-bounded lesson components rather than add-ons, making routine use the path of least resistance.
  • If lesson components are digital, AI-generated discussion prompts and real-time student thinking alerts that reduce the translation burden between curriculum materials and live classroom needs requires further testing to determine viability — as does the live coaching cues above.
For Students, This Means
Classwork problems that hold their cognitive demand and teacher supports that protect struggle rather than remove it.
The underlying logic: the decision to scaffold, press, or step back is currently an in-the-moment expertise demand. Externalizing that decision logic at the problem level converts it into a structured, followable choice that preserves both teacher confidence and student cognitive load.
Validation Signals in Strawman 4.0
  • Frequency of teacher scaffolding vs. press/probe moves during problem-solving (observation coding scheme)
  • Rate of premature answer-giving before productive struggle (observer-coded lesson data)
  • Implementation fidelity ratings: observed vs. intended lesson execution (observation protocol)
  • Frequency of teacher responses to student misconceptions during lesson (observation protocol)
  • Rates of student-to-student talk and use of mathematical reasoning language (observation protocol)
  • Teacher self-reported confidence in real-time instructional decisions (pre/post survey)
  • Routine launch rate per lesson across 30, 60, and 90 school days (observation protocol)
  • Student's ability to take the next step forward after the teacher move (observation protocol)
Problem: Pacing, readiness gaps, and the absence of scalable coaching.

Teachers feel stuck between two options: keep pace or stop to address gaps. Without guidance built into the curriculum, most end up improvising, slowing down, skipping lessons, or rushing through content. Districts face the same bind with professional learning. Funding and time are limited, so some teachers get a heavy lift of professional learning up front and little after that. Research is clear that this rarely sticks, and we do not currently have a scalable solution to support professional learning over time.

Student Experience
Students who enter a unit with prerequisite gaps either sit through content they cannot yet access or fall further behind during unplanned re-teaching stretches. Students in districts with limited coaching resources receive fewer course corrections over the year. By the time a gap compounds into a visible performance problem, multiple instructional opportunities have already passed.
Hypothesis
If MH2 builds diagnostic checkpoints, prerequisite supports, and ongoing professional learning directly into the instructional experience, teachers stop having to choose between keeping pace and meeting students where they are, and students with prerequisite gaps get targeted support before those gaps compound into something harder to fix. This means:
  • Curriculum-integrated diagnostic checkpoints at content focus shifts, keyed to the knowledge graph's pivotal progressions.
  • Prerequisite micro-supports built into the lesson sequence as low-disruption on-ramps, not detours.
  • A scalable hybrid human-AI coaching model, possibly Co-teach, that delivers personalized, embedded coaching regardless of district resources.
  • In-product coaching nudges and lesson-level pedagogical rationale, reinforcing the connection between instructional intent and classroom action throughout the year. An AI-powered reflection prompt tied to actual classroom moments needs more testing to determine if it is a viable solution for G3+.
For Students, This Means
  • A path to grade-level content that does not ask their teacher to choose between pacing and readiness.
  • A feedback loop fast enough to interrupt confusion before it becomes avoidance.
The underlying logic: relying solely on front-loaded professional learning rarely produces lasting change. In addition to front-loaded professional learning, embedding support at the point of practice, and continuously throughout the year, replaces the dependency on external professional development with a curriculum that teaches teachers how to use it while they are using it.
Validation Signals in Strawman 4.0
  • Rate of pacing guide adherence at 30/60/90 days (teacher log + usage data)
  • Frequency of unplanned re-teaching episodes per module (teacher self-report + observation)
  • Time-on-task for grade-level content vs. prerequisite content within a lesson (classroom observation)
  • Implementation fidelity scores at 30, 60, and 90 days post-adoption (observation protocol)
  • Rate of curriculum supplementation or lesson modification over time (teacher self-report + usage data)
  • Teacher engagement with in-product coaching features (feature usage analytics)
  • Teacher confidence ratings on curriculum-specific practices (pre/post survey, repeated at mid-year)
  • Reduction in variability in instructional quality across schools (inter-rater observation data)
  • Rating of the student experience (post survey)
Problem: Teachers often default to lecture-based instruction and an "I Do-We Do-You Do" format instead of research-based explicit instruction.
Student Experience
In a lecture-heavy classroom, students spend most of the lesson listening rather than thinking out loud. They rarely get to explain their reasoning, compare strategies with a classmate, or build on someone else's idea. Over time, this narrows what students think math class is for: watching and copying, not reasoning and arguing. Because competencies related to meaningful discourse are not explicitly taught, students don't develop the skills, like listening to understand or communicating their thinking, that let them participate with confidence. The students who don't naturally jump into whole-class talk stay quiet, and their thinking never enters the room. Over time, this shapes how students see themselves as math learners: capable of following along, but not of contributing.
Hypothesis
If MH2 replaces the default I Do-We Do-You Do structure with curriculum-embedded discourse routines, explicit instruction in student participation skills, and a fixed introduce-practice-fade arc for scaffolds, then teachers will move away from lecture-based delivery and toward instruction that gives students sustained opportunities to explain, justify, compare, and build on each other's thinking. This means:
  • We name this as a point where we will provide explicit implementation support into what effective explicit instruction is, rather than "teacher lecture," grounded in the discourse recommendations for Strawman 4.0.
  • Facilitation supports embedded at the point of use, such as anticipated responses, talk moves, and slide cues, so teachers aren't improvising discourse in real time.
  • Low-barrier entry points and participation scaffolds, such as sentence stems and partner talk before whole group, so discourse isn't dominated by a few students.
  • Explicit instruction in discourse competencies, such as listening to understand and communicating mathematical thinking, taught with the same rigor as content.
  • A small set of predictable routines that grow in complexity across grade bands, rather than a single default format.
  • Discourse built into the rhythm of every lesson, not treated as an occasional add-on.
The underlying logic: I Do-We Do-You Do persists not because teachers reject discourse, but because it's the lower-effort, more predictable path when facilitation is left entirely to in-the-moment expertise. Open discourse asks teachers to generate anticipated responses, sequencing decisions, and talk moves on the fly, and lecture avoids all of that. Embedding those supports directly in the curriculum removes the trade-off. High-fidelity discourse becomes the easier path instead of the harder one — the same shift the discourse paper makes for facilitation burden generally.
Validation Signals in Strawman 4.0
  • Observed instructional format per lesson: I Do-We Do-You Do vs. discourse routine (observation protocol)
  • Teacher use of embedded talk moves and facilitation supports (observation coding scheme)
  • Distribution of student participation, including whose ideas are taken up and built on (observation protocol)
  • Teacher-reported confidence facilitating discourse routines vs. defaulting to lecture (pre/post survey)
  • Fidelity to the introduce-practice-fade arc: whether supports are actually fading on schedule (observation protocol)
  • Ratio of student talk to teacher talk during discourse moments (observation protocol)
Needs further investigation: the discourse paper notes that the link between regularity and predictability of discourse moments and learning outcomes has not been directly tested. This should be validated on its own rather than assumed, since it underpins the "embed discourse into the rhythm of every lesson" design principle this hypothesis depends on.

Warm Up

The first 5 minutes of a lesson. Activates prior knowledge and builds procedural skill through immediate feedback, offering a point of success and a window into student thinking before instruction begins. Non-digital delivery builds a shared whole-class experience; digital and application-problem formats open up at grade 3+ once transition costs are minimized.

  • Build ReadinessGain confidence through a consistent routine that activates prior knowledge; K–5 builds foundational number sense and fact automaticity.
  • Belonging & FeedbackFeel belonging through whole-group or partner activities, and get immediate feedback from the teacher — peer feedback appropriate in 3–5.
  • ConnectionConnect the warm-up to the day's learning, especially explicit in grades 3–5; in 6+, variety in format serves a role similar to interleaving.
  • Assess ReadinessObserve students before instruction begins — read the room, notice who's settled, who needs connection, what the class needs to be ready to think.
  • ChoiceK–2 uses highly specified materials with minimal choice; 3–5 allows limited choice among 2–3 matched routines; 6+ hands teachers full choice.

New Learning

Called "Learning" in the source document. Builds on prior learning through an intentional, teacher-facilitated sequence of activities. Students work collaboratively with peers and the teacher through explicit instruction, structured discourse, guided exploration, and problem solving. Natural checkpoints support formative assessment and feedback, and every lesson closes with summarization.

  • EngageParticipate in a structured activity — partner task, group problem, or teacher-led experience — grounding learning in doing before defining.
  • Discuss (required)Read a problem and converse to surface strategies, reveal reasoning, and build toward a solution together.
  • Analyze WorkExamine correct, partial, or erroneous work samples to sharpen understanding.
  • Summarize (required)Consolidate learning in their own words and carry key ideas into practice. Can happen at the end of learning, and again at the end of the lesson (typically after practice).
  • Conduct Explicit InstructionMake real-time decisions — name strategies, clarify misconceptions.
  • Facilitate DiscourseLead structured talk moves, using embedded if-then decision trees to navigate misconceptions.
  • AssessmentCapture evidence of understanding via the Digital Observational Assessment tool, informed by informal observation and student practice results.
  • Develop Student IdentityMake deliberate choices about whose thinking is elevated; build confidence and belonging.
  • Also (secondary)Build relationships, promote a positive classroom atmosphere, and stamp the big ideas at the close.
For Brand New Learning: Launch Activity, Whole Group, Exploration
For Broadening: Collaboration, Application, Modeling
For Summarizing: Consolidation, Naming the Learning, Checking for Misconceptions, Transfer Setup

Practice

Students apply and refine mathematical procedures and reasoning, building accuracy, retention, and the ability to transfer skills. They work independently or with peers, receive feedback (digital or teacher-delivered), and use it to refine their approach. The teacher circulates, captures evidence of understanding, and decides whether to pull a small group or adjust pacing.

  • In-Class PracticeComplete practice alone, with a partner, or in a small group; a Target Problem doubles as an assessment that's invisible to the student as a separate task.
  • Out-of-Class PracticeEvery set includes Lesson Practice, and — depending on grade — Fluency and Spiral Review, with spacing that widens across the year.
  • Circulate & ObserveSpot patterns, note misconceptions, and capture evidence through the DOARS tool.
  • DecideUse that evidence — plus prior data and embedded supports — to run small-group instruction or adjust pacing.

Topics roll up into a module

A module strings several topics together.
